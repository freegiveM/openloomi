# Global Managers

A manager running a team that lives in three time zones — Singapore, Berlin, San Francisco — opens Slack at 9 AM in San Francisco to find that the Singapore teammates have already finished their day, the Berlin team is two hours from lunch, and the overnight pings from Tokyo are piled in three different inboxes.

Every morning used to start the same way: Gmail is opened, Slack is opened, Lark is opened, scrolling continues for 30 minutes trying to figure out what was missed, and only then does the actual work begin. By the time catch-up is complete, half the morning is gone. And worse — the act of catching up generates more messages ("sorry, just saw this"), which the team then has to triage on top of their own work.

The shortfall isn't discipline. It's the cognitive load of holding three time zones in mind at the same time.

The user doesn't want another chatbot that waits to be asked. They want an AI assistant that genuinely fits a manager's day across time zones: it polls every channel continuously, organizes the overnight signal, and only finds them when there's a decision that genuinely needs to be made.

---

## Problem 1: The APAC / EMEA gap eats mornings

The painful part of being a global manager isn't the late-night Slack pings — it's the morning archaeology to figure out which of them actually matter. The exchange used to go like this:

- Open Slack and scroll the unread list, channel by channel
- Try to remember which of the 40 unread messages were just chat and which needed a reply
- Open Gmail and check if anything landed overnight from investors or partners
- Open Lark and check if the Berlin team left decisions in threads
- Stitch the morning's mental picture together from memory

If the night was busy, this got pushed to 10 AM, sometimes 11. By the time catch-up is complete, the Singapore team is already past lunch and Berlin is on their way out.

This is exactly what AI should be doing. The actual hard part of the morning routine isn't the decisions — it's gathering the scattered overnight signal and reorganizing it into something a manager can act on.

So the three primary channels are connected in OpenLoomi:

```
use openloomi-connectors to connect Gmail
use openloomi-connectors to connect Slack
use openloomi-connectors to connect Lark
```

Gmail and Slack live in Composio's app catalog; the seven native Loomi connectors (Telegram / WhatsApp / iMessage / Feishu / DingTalk / QQ / WeChat) don't need Composio at all. Native and Composio-managed accounts can be mixed freely.

Verification runs with `list-accounts` — once every platform shows `active`, Loop picks them up on the next tick.

## Problem 2: 9 AM becomes a triage ritual, not a start of day

Once the connectors are in, the next thing configured is the **Brief**. Every day at 9 AM (configurable in **Settings → Loop**), Loop gathers the open decision cards, today's calendar, and the contacts Loop expects an interaction with today, and lays them out as a single page.

What lands in the Brief isn't a hardcoded count of "3 items" — it's everything Loop judged the previous day and overnight that genuinely needs attention. If nothing needs the manager, the Brief says so.

The Brief isn't the only thing that runs on a schedule. **Throughout the day**, Loop ticks every 10 minutes and surfaces typed decision cards as new signals clear the gates:

- ✉️ **`email_reply`** — inbound from a contact on the important list with an action word, past the reply window
- 💬 **`im_reply`** — DM from a connected messaging app not yet responded to
- 📅 **`rsvp`** — calendar invite past 24 hours unanswered

Each card carries the draft, a reasoning trace, the confidence score, and the four-gesture flow — **Approve / Edit Draft / Later / Skip**. The dry-run is always shown before any outbound action — Loop never auto-sends.

By the time the laptop is opened, the overnight queue is already filtered. The Brief is read, **Approve** is tapped on the two or three things that genuinely need attention, and the day starts in 5 minutes instead of 45.

## Problem 3: 6 PM becomes a guilt trip

The mirror problem to the morning Brief was the evening Wrap. Used to be: logging off, lying in bed, and thinking _"did I reply to that Singapore thread? Did I confirm the Berlin call? Did I acknowledge the partner's note?"_ If tired, Slack would be opened one more time to check, and that one more time would turn into 30 minutes of "while I'm here..."

Loop's **Wrap** at 6 PM (configurable in **Settings → Loop**) lists what Loop actually handled during the day — emails sent, RSVPs logged, replies posted — and what got parked for tomorrow. The goal is a clean mental inbox before logging off.

## What this workflow really solves isn't a few automation tasks

At first the goal was just to save the 45 minutes every morning being spent on overnight archaeology. But after a few weeks, the realization hit: the real problem isn't a few missing automation scripts — it's that global-team work is fundamentally a time-zone information problem:

- The team lives in three time zones; the working day only covers one of them
- Async handoffs mean yesterday's decisions are buried in threads that haven't been seen
- The cost of context-loading a 200-message overnight Slack scroll is real
- By the time something is noticed, it's already 4–6 hours old in the other time zone

In the past, all this information had to be found and pieced together by actively searching. The change OpenLoomi brings is inverting that relationship: instead of waking up and trying to catch up, AI keeps watching every channel continuously and comes back with the filtered list at the right moment.

- If a Tokyo teammate DMs at 11 PM their time, the `im_reply` card lands in the Brief already knowing who they are.
- If a partner emails overnight, the `email_reply` card surfaces with the draft grounded in the last conversation.
- If a calendar invite sits unanswered past 24 hours, the `rsvp` card waits in the morning's queue.
- If a public signal changes a contact's context, the `contact_update` card refreshes memory overnight.

All that's needed is to focus on the parts that genuinely need judgement — the actual reply, the RSVP, the decision.

## From "morning triage" to a continuously running manager loop

To make this work, two kinds of working patterns are configured in OpenLoomi.

**One is event-driven:**

| Trigger                                                | What happens                                                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Inbound email / IM past reply window                   | `email_reply` or `im_reply` card with draft grounded in memory and reasoning trace |
| Calendar invite past 24 hours unanswered               | `rsvp` card with the response options                                              |
| Public signal on a contact (promotion, new role, etc.) | `contact_update` card proposes the memory refresh                                  |

**The other is scheduled:**

| Task                      | Schedule          | Purpose                                                          |
| ------------------------- | ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Today's To-Do (Brief)** | Every day at 9 AM | Pulls today's calendar + open decision cards + relevant contacts |
| **Today's Done (Wrap)**   | Every day at 6 PM | Lists what Loop handled for you today + what got parked          |

Together they form a loop that runs continuously across every time zone:

```
Continuously watch → Organize contact context → Propose action or draft → Human decides → Send and remember
```

Loop never sleeps. Brief and Wrap are the bookends; the queue runs in between. Compared to configuring an isolated per-channel workflow, the preference is to have OpenLoomi keep understanding _"what landed across all the time zones since last looked."_

> 💡 **Tune what surfaces.** Global managers often have more signals than time. Three dials help: **Trusted contacts** (the auto-promote list, default ≥ 3 appearances in memory), **Sources** (toggle Email / Calendar / GitHub / Slack / Linear individually in **Settings → Loop → Sources**), and **Quiet hours** (pause windows around deep-work blocks in **Settings → Attention Agent**).

## How to set this up

Three connector calls is all it takes. The same commands work in OpenLoomi Desktop, the Claude plugin, or the Codex plugin — `openloomi-connectors` is invoked in chat, once per channel:

```
use openloomi-connectors to connect Gmail
use openloomi-connectors to connect Slack
use openloomi-connectors to connect Lark
```

Verification runs with `list-accounts` — once every platform shows `active`, Loop picks them up on the next tick.

From that moment on:

- Every 10 min Loop polls all the channels and surfaces typed cards as signals clear the gates
- The Brief lands at 9 AM (or the configured time); the Wrap lands at 6 PM
- Memory auto-promotes frequent contacts into the trusted list
- Loomi bubbles the high-confidence cards in the corner of the screen during the day

Trusted contacts, source toggles, and brief/wrap times are tuned in **Settings → Loop**. Loomi's bubble cadence, quiet hours, and theme are in **Settings → Attention Agent**. If the Brief is wanted in a different time zone or only in certain languages, the time is set in **Settings → Loop → Morning briefing time**; signal prioritisation respects the Interests panel in **Settings → Personalization**.

OpenLoomi's audit log records every execution — how many times Loop has polled, which cards surfaced, which Approves were tapped, which drafts were edited. This way the overnight triage can be handed off to AI while every action it took remains visible.
